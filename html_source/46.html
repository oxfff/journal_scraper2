      • Peer victimization linked to negative affect in children with and without ADHD

        Original Research Article
      • Pages 1-10
      • Nicholas D. Fogleman, Danielle M. Walerius, Paul J. Rosen, Kirsten D. Leaberry
        • Abstract
        • Close research highlights
        •  PDF (412 K)

        Highlights

        •

        Explored relationship between peer victimization and affect in children with and without ADHD

        •

        Children and parents were administered measures of peer victimization and child affect.

        •

        Negative affect was uniquely related to peer victimization in both child- and parent-report.

        •

        Parent-report of peer victimization was moderated by child ADHD diagnostic status.

        •

        Negative affect plays an important role in peer victimization in children with and without ADHD.

      • Relationship of TV watching, computer use, and reading to children's neurocognitive functions

        Original Research Article
      • Pages 11-21
      • Johanna Rosenqvist, Pekka Lahti-Nuuttila, James Holdnack, Sally L. Kemp, Marja Laasonen
        • Abstract
        • Close research highlights
        •  PDF (333 K)

        Highlights

        •

        We studied the relationship between media use and neurocognitive performance.

        •

        TV watching was negatively related to neurocognition.

        •

        Computer use was positively related to neurocognition.

        •

        Reading was positively related to neurocognition in younger children.

        •

        Maternal education was the strongest predictor.

      • Does social–behavioral adjustment mediate the relation between executive function and academic readiness?

        Original Research Article
      • Pages 22-30
      • Joana Baptista, Ana Osório, Eva Costa Martins, Manuela Verissimo, Carla Martins
        • Abstract
        • Close research highlights
        •  PDF (387 K)

        Highlights

        •

        We analyzed whether executive function was related to academic readiness.

        •

        We tested whether social–emotional adjustment mediated the above relation.

        •

        Better executive function predicted greater academic readiness.

        •

        Social–behavioral adjustment acted as a mediator in the link above.

        •

        A full mediation was observed.

        Original Research Article
      • Pages 31-40
      • Martin Van Boekel, Okan Bulut, Luke Stanke, Jose R. Palma Zamora, Yoojeong Jang, Youngsoon Kang, Kyle Nickodem
        • Abstract
        • Close research highlights
        •  PDF (366 K)

        Highlights

        •

        Propensity score matching was employed to study the benefits of participating in school-organized sports.

        •

        Participation in school sports is associated with higher GPAs.

        •

      • Patterns of anxiety symptoms during adolescence: Gender differences and sociomotivational factors

        Original Research Article
      • Pages 41-50
      • Stéphane Duchesne, Catherine F. Ratelle
        • Abstract
        • Close research highlights
        •  PDF (481 K)

        Highlights

        •

        Longitudinal sample: 493 adolescents with six waves between age 11 to 16 years

        •

        Three comparable trajectory patterns were found for boys and girls.

        •

        A subgroup of girls reported anxiety symptoms that increased over time.

        •

        Sociomotivational factors emerged as determinants for these developmental patterns.

        •

        Findings have implications for the design of preventive interventions in school.

      • Teacher-child relationships and friendships and peer victimization across the school year

        Original Research Article
      • Pages 63-72
      • Marina Serdiouk, Daniel Berry, Scott D. Gest
        • Abstract
        • Close research highlights
        •  PDF (490 K)

        Highlights

        •

        We tested within-/between-person effects of teacher-child relationships/friendships.

        •

        Within- and between-person effects varied in magnitude and direction.

        •

        Children with more friends/better teacher-child relationships were victimized less.

        •

        Within-person teacher-child relationship effect was positive in early fall.

        •

        No within-person effect of friendship on victimization was evident.
